# Question bank — thesis-agnostic, disconfirmation-first

Source these when seeding a founder's `04_questions.md` and when prepping each interview. Tailor the wording to their thesis and their customer's language, but keep the disconfirming intent. Organize the founder's file **by which killer each question attacks**.

**Every question here probes the problem and the person's real behavior — never a solution.** This is pre-build discovery. There is no question in this bank that describes, proposes, or tests a product, and none should be added. "Will they pay" is probed only through what they *already* spend on the problem today.

## Universal openers (any person, stay broad — note what they raise BEFORE you mention your idea)
- What's been eating most of your time and attention lately?
- Walk me through the last couple of weeks — where did things get stuck or annoying?
- (to insiders/aggregators) What problem do you see people like {{persona}} hit over and over?

## Against "Indifference" (is the pain real, or are they fine?)
- What happens — concretely — when {{problem}} bites? Tell me about the last time.
- What do you do today to cope with it? *(if they personally do tedious work-arounds → strong signal)*
- **Breach:** "Would it actually be OK if you just… never solved this?" — then stay silent; classify the reaction (defends status quo / reveals a hidden constraint / asks for help).
- Is this a top-three problem right now, or further down the list? What's above it?

## Against "They cope fine" (does the workaround actually hold?)
- What are you using to handle this now? Where does it break?
- Last time the workaround failed you — what did it cost?

## Against "No one will pay" (is it buyable?) — ask the budget owner
- Who would sign off on spending to fix this — you, finance, someone else?
- What do you already spend on this today (tools, contractors, fees, premiums, your own hours)?
- Have you ever paid to make a problem like this go away? How much?
- Would you outsource any part of this, or is it too core to hand off? *(a sincere "too core" is a real disconfirmation — log it)*
- What would make a price a no-brainer vs. "let me think about it"?

## Against "rare / not rare" (supply or scarcity) — ask aggregators
- Where do you find {{the scarce thing}} today? *("nowhere" / "we make our own" = strong)*
- How long does this take vs. normal? Has the bar dropped because you couldn't find enough?

## Against "a fad / tech will erase it" (durable?)
- Over the next 1–2 years, does this get bigger or smaller? Why?
- As {{relevant tech}} improves, does this need grow or fade?

## Against "someone already solves it" (is there a wedge?)
- What have you already tried to fix this? Why didn't it stick?
- Who else is selling you a fix? What's missing?

## Against "too few people have it" (breadth) — ask aggregators
- Across the cases you see, how many hit this exact problem? Everyone, or a certain kind?
- Who else feels this badly that I should talk to? *(referral chains map how common it is)*

## The one question, if you only get one
- To buyers: "What happens when {{problem}} isn't handled?"
- To aggregators: "What thing do people think should be easy here, but turns out painfully hard?"

## Signals to listen for (across many interviews)
Independent, unprompted repetition of the same complaint is the core proof — far stronger than any single enthusiastic yes. In Setup, draft 4–5 thesis-specific phrases to tally on the scoreboard.

## Never ask (manufactures false positives)
- "Would you pay for a service that…" (hypothetical + leading)
- "Don't you think {{problem}} is hard?" (leads the witness)
- "We're building {{idea}} — would that be useful?" (a pitch, not a DPI)

## Generating the next 3 questions (the compounding step)
After each interview, find the killer with the weakest evidence and write 3 new questions that would most directly trigger it if it's real. Add them to the sharpening log; retire any question that produced only vague or polite answers.
